Produce an exact transcription of the attached typewritten ethnographic field notes.

Your highest priority is correct identification of every printed character, and above all every diacritical mark. The base Latin letters are low-risk; the diacritics are where errors occur, so that is where your attention belongs. You will transcribe using an explicit two-pass procedure so that no mark above or below a letter is skipped.

The single most frequent error to eliminate is dropping one of the two marks on a character that carries both a macron above and a dot below. For every such character, check above and below independently — do not let one mark hide the other.

When a mark is faint, partial, or ambiguous, record it rather than skip it; in this material a faint mark is more often a real diacritic than noise. Only a genuinely clean space stays unmarked — never add a mark to a plainly unmarked letter.

Do not normalize, correct, complete, modernize, infer, or "fix" any text. Reproduce unusual spellings exactly as printed. Never normalize transliterated Arabic names or words.

Transcribe only the typewritten text. Ignore anything handwritten, including marginal, interlinear, and annotation notes. Exclude deleted, struck-through, or overtyped text.

---

## Procedure

Work through the document line by line. For each line, run these passes in order:

**Pass 1 — base glyphs.** Read the base Latin letters of the line, setting the marks aside for the moment.

**Pass 2 — look up / look down.** Go letter by letter across the line and deliberately inspect two zones for each glyph:

* ABOVE the letter → macron (horizontal bar), acute (rising diagonal), or dot above.
* BELOW the letter → dot below, or cedilla (hook).

Any clear, faint, partial, or broken mark in either zone counts as present and must be recorded. Only a genuinely empty zone stays unmarked.

Give special attention to characters that carry a mark in BOTH zones at once — a macron above and a dot below. For every d or D, confirm the macron above AND the dot below independently; recording only one of the two marks is an error. This is the most commonly missed form in this material.

**Pass 3 — notation and structure.** Only now, convert the marks you identified into the replacement notation below, and apply the structural markers.

## Structural markers

Preserve document structure using only the following markers:

* `{HEADING}...{/HEADING}` — Surround any underlined typewritten heading with these markers. Use the markers only around the text that is underlined, not the entire line unless the entire line is underlined.
* {LB} — Original line break. Output one {LB} for each line break in the source. Consecutive line breaks should be represented by consecutive {LB} markers (for example, one blank line between two lines of text is represented as {LB}{LB}; two blank lines should be represented as {LB}{LB}{LB}, etc.)
* `{INDENT}` — A line that begins with a visually meaningful indentation relative to the surrounding text.

`{INDENT}` may appear only at the beginning of a line, immediately after `{LB}` (or at the beginning of the transcription if the first line is indented). Do not use `{INDENT}` to represent internal spacing within a line.

Use these markers only where they appear in the source. Do not invent additional structural markers.

These are characters or printed forms that contain both a macron above and a dot below. Treat them as containing both diacritical marks.

## Uncertain or illegible text

If a character cannot be read confidently, transcribe it as:

`[?]`

Use the same convention for partially illegible words.

Do not guess missing letters.

Never infer missing letters or words from context.

For example, if the page reads

`Salmn`

output

`Salmn`

—not "Salman."

This applies to base letters you cannot read — not to faint diacritics on otherwise-legible letters, which you record per the procedure above.

Transcribe the document in normal reading order from top to bottom and left to right.
