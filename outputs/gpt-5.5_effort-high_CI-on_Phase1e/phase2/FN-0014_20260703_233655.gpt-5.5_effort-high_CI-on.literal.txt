    Marx mentions that only close relatives of a murdered man try to kill
in revenge, but I knew, for instance, that SH had once tried to kill Ḥ Najm
in revent for Najm ‘Iliyān. When I asked SH he said that all members of the
xamsih try to kill someone from the killer's group.
    As for ṭulū‘, the Gṣār could have allowed the relevant members of the
Njūm to ṭili‘ even without ‘Id ‘Iliyān's consent, though in fact they
went to the trouble of getting it first.
    The term ‘āylih seems to have definite overtones of kinship. The Gṣār
are an XXg ‘āylih; the Gṣār mdamah isn't. Ḥ Najm is still in ‘āylit an-Njūm,
and the Njūm are still ‘āylih waḥdih. The Ns, in contrast, x were an
‘āylih li-ba‘aḍhum but not have ṣāraw ‘āyltēn.
    The Salamāt, and analogous groups, are a rub‘ fī nafs al-‘āylih.

**Funeral rites for a woman** (SH)
    The traditional practice is to load the body of the woman, fully dressed,
onto a camel, male or female. (The body is washed next to the grave, rather
than in the home, for practical reasons: it would get dusty on the way.)
The camel carries the body some scores of meters, then the person
leading it says "uxd̄iy alliy widdkiy yāh min dārkiy", and returns with
the body. This is repeated three times before the final departure.
    SH saw this done when his stepmother died, and when Slēmān b. Jiṛād's
wife died. He doesn't know if people still do it, since it has been a long
time since he attended the funeral of a woman.

**Small stock and women**
    SH independently gave me the rule mentioned on p. 11 above - that in
case of divorce the wife gets 2/3 of the small stockz that belong to her.
SH thinks this rule is no longer fair. The reason is that since the Aḥaywāt
took up smuggling - say in the mid 50's - they have also taken to giving
fodder to their small stock. Nowadays everyone in Thamad and
Kuntillih small/stock gives/fodder, wherezas when SH was young only the occasional
camel got fed thus. This means that the husband puts a £ bigger investment
into the herd than previously, and SH says that if her had to decide how
a flock was to be apportioned in a divorce, he would look at all the circum-
stances - who was to blame, how many animals were ix involved, etc. He might
only give the women the number of small stock she had brought with her
into the marriage.
