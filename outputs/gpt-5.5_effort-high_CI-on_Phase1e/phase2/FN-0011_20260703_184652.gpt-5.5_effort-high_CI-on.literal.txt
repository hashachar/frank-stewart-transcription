**Inheritance**
    **Small stock.** My information on this is really not satisfactory, and I
shall have to look at some actual flocks and find out just who owns what.
However, it is clear that everybody in the family owns some small stock.*
Thus when the father dies, only a small part of the flock has to be
divided up. I think it would probably be divided equally between his wife
and children. [*Slēm has given his two little sons stock from his flock
(not from his mother's).]
    A girl his given her share of the small stock on marriage, or rather,
about a month after she marries, when it is apparent that the marriage is
going to continue at least into the immediate future. Each time Fuṭūm married,
it seems, she took half a dozen or so small stock - and each time she was
divorced she brought them back. However, Slēm also says that on divorce the
flock which the woman brings into the marriage is divided, she get-
ting 2/3 and the husband 1/3, since he paid for feeding them etc. If there
are children, they keep whatever they have been given of her small stock,
but get no more.
    In talking aboutt this subject, Slēm several times emphasized the rights
that accrue to a person by virtue of working with or paying for food for
stock.
    **Camels.** At the time that Slēm married each of the three brothers owned
two camels. The women (mother and daughter) had none.
    **Land.** Also equally divided between sons. Ṣāliḥ left several plots in
W. al-'Agābih. Each of these plots was divided into three parts, one for
each son.
    **Money.** This is not something that usually arises, but I said: imagine
a man died after h'd just sold a lorry, and had, say IL 50,000 in cash. Slēm
said it would go equally to all the children (m and f). A woman would only
get a share as long as she remained unmarried and with her children. In
contrast, her'right to her small stock is absolute: they go with her if she
remarries.
