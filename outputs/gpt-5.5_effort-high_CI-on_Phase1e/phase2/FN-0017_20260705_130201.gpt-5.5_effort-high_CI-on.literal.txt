Field notes        17

    Three issues came up, and were dealt with one by one. The first was
the accusation that Sālim ‘Id had offered IL 50,000 for the killing of
‘Id Farrāj. Sālim offered either biś‘ih or oath on this, and did so so
convincingly that in fact he was asked to give neither, and the other two
accusations were dismissed almost automatically: one was that/‘Iliy or Mismiḥ ‘Id
had tried to get Slēmān ‘Iliyān to kill ‘Id and to get Rayya’ to do
the same, the other the matter of ‘Abd ar-Ra‘ūf (which last seems to be true).
    There seems no doubt that everything young Slēmān ‘Iliyān told his
father was lies (except the ‘Abd ar-Ra‘ūf episode). He was supported in these
lies by his sister Rayya’. It seems that his mother (Fāṭmih b. al-‘Umdih)
put him up to it. He (or she) was angry because the Ns tried to frustrate
his plans to marry his sister to the Graygāniyīn.
    Much remains unclear and I'll have to go over the tapes. The whole
matter was settled by 6.30. The Ns were given an award (I forget exactly
what, a couple of camels I think) which they forwent.
    Rayya’ has been daxaled by Sālim ‘Id to SI, Sallām ‘Iliyān and Ḥ. Najm.
Sālim has not xaṭṭ 9, but has offered (barrak) rbā‘ al-jīrih and to pay
all her expenses henceforth (though she remains with her family). This seems
to be a standard move: SH did this in his dispute with Ḥamdān ‘Id, and he and
‘Id ‘Iliyān did it in about 1962 when they daxaled the sister of ‘Iliyān Ibn
Mismiḥ. Her F, ‘Iliy the blind, wanted to marry her to someone in Jordan
.(Ibn Farrāj of the Ṭhaytiyyih, who had her gaṣalah); but at the time ‘Id
‘Iliyān wasn’t married, and so objected. After he married, about a year later,
he rafa‘ ‘inha ad-daxal.
    If the father accepts the offer of jīrih and maṣrūf - and SH knows
of no cases where he has - then the person who has made the offer controls
the marriage of the girl henceforth.
    What would happen if ‘Iliyān Ibn Mismiḥ married his daughter to the
Graygāniyīn in spite of the daxal? Sālim ‘Id would gawwam the daxala, and
they would each get a manṣad wijh from ‘Iliyān. Furthermore, the judge would
say "ana min ‘indī, al-gānūn sāriy ‘a ṭalāgha." This would give the Ns the
right to seize her and to keep her till her husband divorced her. Note that
in this kind of case the judge does not himself effect the divorce, in con-
trast to the case where the H has to pay a manṣad for an offence against his
W. In such a case the judge says "ana min ‘indī inha mṭallagah" before he
awards the manṣad, and these words effect the divorce. The kifīl of the divorce
in such as case is, naturally, the kifīl of the ḥagg as a whole.
    After settling the matter we drove back to Ṭlā‘it Ṣarrāḥ (with Ibn
Mismiḥ) and there spent the night. Sallām and Ṣāliḥ ‘Umdih were present, and
there was a lot of talk whirch I recorded.
